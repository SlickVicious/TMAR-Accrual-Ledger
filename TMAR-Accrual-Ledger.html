THE ARBITRATION AWARD AS FINANCIAL ASSET AND CASH EQUIVALENT
A final arbitration award, once issued and no longer subject to modification or vacatur under the Federal Arbitration Act (43 Stat. 883 §§9-12), establishes a legally enforceable obligation for payment that constitutes property in the hands of the creditor. Awards issued under valid, written arbitration agreements are governed by the FAA Sections 1-2. The parties contractually agreed that arbitration outcomes would be final and binding. No motions to vacate, modify, or correct were filed within the statutory 90-day period (FAA §§10-12). More than 300 days elapsed, making the awards final, binding, and uncontested by law.

The awards represent permanent, enforceable judgments between the parties without the need for judicial confirmation under §9, because no party sought such confirmation within the statutory one-year window. Judicial confirmation is NOT mandatory — it is a separate, optional enforcement mechanism. The award IS the judgment between the parties by contractual agreement and operation of law.

Baltimore & Ohio R.R. Co. v. United States, 261 U.S. 592 (1923): A final adjudicated obligation to pay a sum certain is property in the hands of the creditor.

Board of Regents v. Roth, 408 U.S. 564 (1972): Any legally enforceable claim to money constitutes property within the meaning of law and is protected under the Fifth Amendment.

Under GAAP (ASC 305-10-20), an asset qualifies as a cash equivalent when its value is readily determinable and it is readily convertible to known amounts of cash without significant risk of change in value. After the statutory contest period has expired (90 days per 43 Stat. 885 §12), the award becomes final and non-appealable, fixes a definite monetary amount, bears no further contingencies, and is immediately collectible.

Spring City Foundry Co. v. Commissioner, 292 U.S. 182 (1934): When the right to receive income becomes fixed, the income accrues, although not then actually received.

PROPERTY-BASED TAX PAYMENT AUTHORITY
Revenue Act of 1918, §1307, 40 Stat. 1143: "Taxes shall be paid in money, or in obligations having a fair market value equivalent thereto."

The word "shall" is mandatory language — Runkle v. United States, 122 U.S. 543 (1887). The word "or" creates alternatives of equal validity. Property payment is not subordinate to cash payment — both are authorized methods.

Old Colony Trust Co. v. Commissioner, 279 U.S. 716 (1929): When a liability is discharged through the use of property having ascertainable value, the discharge is treated as payment in money.

Helvering v. Bruun, 309 U.S. 461 (1940): Transfer of property or other valuable right in satisfaction of a tax or debt constitutes realization and payment at its fair market value.

United States v. Kirby Lumber Co., 284 U.S. 1 (1931): Satisfaction of an obligation by property of equivalent value is treated the same as payment in cash.

Under §§202(b)-(c) of the Revenue Acts of 1918 and 1921, §1307 of the Revenue Act of 1918, and the Supreme Court authorities cited above, property having an ascertainable market value may lawfully be used to discharge a tax debt. The IRS lacks authority to refuse statutory payment methods. Chrysler Corp. v. Brown, 441 U.S. 281 (1979): An agency literally has no power to act unless and until Congress confers power upon it.
NET OPERATING LOSSES AS FINANCIAL ASSETS
Revenue Act of 1918, §204(b), 40 Stat. 1060: "The term 'net operating loss' means the excess of the deductions allowed over the gross income." Congress defined losses in exact dollar amounts.

Revenue Act of 1939, §122, 53 Stat. 862: "There SHALL be allowed as a deduction the amount of the net operating loss carry-over." The phrase "shall be allowed" is mandatory, not permissive.

Helvering v. Morgans, Inc., 293 U.S. 121 (1934): The net operating loss is "the arithmetic result of the statutory computation" and must be "applied in full dollar amounts."

Libson Shops, Inc. v. Koehler, 353 U.S. 382 (1957): Congress intended "the actual dollar amount of the loss sustained" to offset future profits.

By statutory language and Supreme Court construction, a net operating loss is a dollar-for-dollar financial asset. Every dollar of documented loss equals one dollar of recognized property value. There is no discount, no present-value calculation, and no reduction factor applied by law.
ACCUMULATED EXPENSE ASSETS
Each documented business expense possessing cash-equivalent character is a financial asset, NOT a deferred tax asset. The accumulated expense asset represents actual documented business expenses that have been incurred and paid, creating a property right with present economic value.

IRREFUTABLE REBUTTALS TO CONTRARY PRESUMPTIONS
PRESUMPTION: Awards Must Be Monetized Before Payment
Revenue Act of 1918, §202(b)-(c) and §1307 explicitly authorize satisfaction of obligations "with property" at fair market value. The statutes do not require conversion to currency as a precondition. Bowers v. Kerbaugh-Empire Co., 271 U.S. 170 (1926) and Old Colony Trust Co. v. Commissioner, 279 U.S. 716 (1929) both hold that property having ascertainable value may be transferred in satisfaction of a debt without first converting it to money.
PRESUMPTION: NOLs Are Not Contractual Rights
Perry v. United States, 294 U.S. 330 (1935): "The United States are as much bound by their contracts as are individuals." When Congress enacts a mandatory deduction using "shall be allowed," it creates an enforceable obligation backed by the sovereign credit. Lynch v. United States, 292 U.S. 571 (1934): "Rights against the United States arising out of its contracts are protected by the Constitution."
PRESUMPTION: Only Cash Can Pay Taxes
Revenue Act of 1918, §1307 expressly states: "Taxes shall be paid in money, or in obligations having a fair market value equivalent thereto." The statute explicitly permits non-cash payment. Helvering v. Bruun, 309 U.S. 461 (1940) held that transfer of property in satisfaction of a tax constitutes payment at fair market value.
PRESUMPTION: Property Payment Requires IRS Pre-Approval
Revenue Act of 1918, §1307 contains no pre-approval requirement. The statute is self-executing. United States v. Shreveport Grain & Elevator Co., 287 U.S. 77 (1932): "Where statutory language is clear and unambiguous, courts must give effect to the plain meaning." Chevron U.S.A. Inc. v. NRDC, 467 U.S. 837 (1984): Administrative agencies may only fill gaps in ambiguous statutes.
PRESUMPTION: Expenses Cannot Become Financial Assets
Under accrual accounting as authorized by Revenue Act of 1918, §212(b), expenses create deductible items. United States v. Kirby Lumber Co., 284 U.S. 1 (1931) and Commissioner v. Tufts, 461 U.S. 300 (1983) both hold that when a liability is discharged, the monetary value constitutes income or realized gain. If discharge creates taxable income for the debtor, then the creditor's documented loss creates a monetary asset with equivalent value.
PRESUMPTION: Statutory Benefits Are Mere Gratuities
Lynch v. United States expressly rejected the gratuity theory: "Valid contracts are property, whether the obligor be a private individual, a municipality, a State, or the United States." Revenue Act language is mandatory: "shall be allowed." When Congress uses imperative language and conditions are met, rights vest. Runkle v. United States, 122 U.S. 543 (1887) establishes that "shall" creates obligation, not discretion.
